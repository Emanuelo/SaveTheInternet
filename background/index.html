Your freedom online is threatened by an EU proposal.
The fight for an open Internet is happening right now in Brussels.


What is going on in the EU?

In September 2013, the European Commission proposed a regulation for a Telecom Single Market (TSM), a legislation that would enshrine net neutrality into European law. However, the Commission text contained several problematic loopholes that would have allowed Internet providers to circumvent net neutrality. Through a provision on 'specialised services', ISPs could have created discriminatory prioritised access. The proposal would also have allowed for discriminatory blocking of websites, applications, and content — a threat to rule of law and freedom of expression.

Fortunately, the European Parliament worked hard to close these loopholes and voted in favour of clear and binding rules for net neutrality on April 3rd of 2014. Although things looked grim at first, your efforts with Savetheinternet helped the Parliament adopt a text with strong net neutrality safeguards. This vote was applauded by millions of people around the world.

After this victory, the EU Member States gathered in the Council and progressed slowly, taking over a year to come up with a joint position. They agreed on a new text that introduced loopholes that would greatly undermine the future of the Internet. If adopted, it would have created paid fast-lanes, allowed price discrimination in the EU, and authorised illegal and arbitrary blocking of lawful content.

With this political agreement from the 28 Member states the negotiations between all three institutions began in the so called "trialogue". In a late night session, at 2am, an agreement was reached between the three institutions which leaves all the important questions unanswered. This new text is ambiguous and contains text that could be used to generate loopholes that would allow discrimination in the Internet. This lack of clarity has to be fixed in the upcoming vote in the European Parliament. This is the last chance for your representative to make a difference in this law and support real net neutrality. There will be amendments tabled for the Plenary vote that we all have to fight for, only then can we protect the Internet and its diversity and innovation that we all came to know in the past decades.

After the vote in the European Parliament, any amendments will either be accepted by the Council and the law can pass or we will enter into another round of trialogue negotiations. When the final text is agreed, the Body of European Regulators of Electronic Communications (BEREC) will make an consultation in which everybody can participate and which will be considered in the creation of Guidelines based on the law adopted by the EU. These guidelines and the law will be binding for the National Regulatory Authorities that govern what telecommunication companies are allowed to do in all 28 member states.
